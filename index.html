Hi! I'm Steven! A Computer Engineer!

I graduated from the University of Florida in 2024 with a bachelor's degree in Computer Engineering. My passion lies in understanding computer systems at their deepest levels—how they operate, how they interface with the physical world, and how they can be engineered for reliability in mission-critical environments.

My interest in computer systems and hardware has lead me to working with Naval Sea Systems Command (NAVSEA), where I currently oversee design and repair of computer systems that operate the nuclear reactors in our various Aircraft Carriers and Submarines. The unique environment of a nuclear powered vessel requires an emphasis on reliability, redundancy, and precision that begins on the hardware and software level, which has strengthened my proficiency in embedded systems, hardware architecture, and real-time operating systems.

My first impactful experience with computers came when I stuck coins from my piggy bank into my mom's iMac CD drive, thinking they would shoot out like a slot machine.
The technician had to explain to 5-year-old me that they would not, in fact, shoot out like a slot machine...

Ever since then, I've been fascinated with the inner workings of computers and how they function.

The major turning point came in high school during a C++ course, when my professor explained that C and C++ were used throughout all layers of computing—from operating systems and compilers down to firmware and BIOS initialization. That realization completely changed how I viewed computers.

Since then, I’ve been especially drawn to low-level systems engineering: embedded programming, digital logic, computer architecture, FPGA development, and the communication between peripherals and processors.

For my senior project, my team and I implemented an NES on an FPGA for a video game museum exhibit. That experience deepened my interest in timing-sensitive hardware, digital design, and system-level engineering.

Today, my interests extend beyond consumer and entertainment hardware into industrial and mission-critical computing systems—particularly those used in energy generation, automation, and naval nuclear infrastructure. I’m especially interested in designing and maintaining systems that must continue operating safely and predictably under demanding real-world conditions.

These projects reflect that curiosity and serve as an ongoing exploration of embedded systems, PCB design, digital electronics, and the engineering principles behind complex critical infrastructure.

Top Projects

Verilog

My top project using Verilog is a PWM controller.

The design operates with an 8-bit counter that toggles between "1" and "0" using a 2-input multiplexer. To change the PWM output frequency, you can latch a new value into the counter and reset the device for the changes to take effect.

VHDL

My top project in VHDL—and the one I'm most proud of—is a 32-bit MIPS-based multi-cycle CPU.

It features memory-mapped I/O with custom memory decoding logic, supports jumping, branching, multiplication, and logical operations, and includes its own built-in RAM—no external RAM required!

My top project in C is my custom system call library.

One of the most fascinating aspects of computers is how we interact with operating systems. But how does a program gain access to resources through the operating system? To explore this, I wrote my own system call library in Linux to better understand this interaction.

C++

What’s better than making any device run DOOM? Making DOOM’s file format mountable as a filesystem in Linux, of course!

This C++ project uses the FUSE library in Linux, along with my custom library, to treat DOOM WAD (Where’s All the Data?) files as their own file directories. This allows you to send a WAD file to anyone, and they can mount it to perform read, write, copy, and other file operations.

Now that’s portability!
